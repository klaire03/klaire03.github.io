Project overview

What this project does

VR meeting spaces are not neutral containers — their materials, lighting, and scale actively shape how users feel and perform. This project asks: which environments feel right, for which meeting types, and which specific design elements are driving those emotional responses?

Source 1 · Customers

Surveys

survey_responses.csv · 108 rows · weight 0.40

Structured open-text responses from customers across six industries. Respondents described how each environment felt, what occasions they'd use it for, and what they'd change. Three text fields per row: feel_text, occasion_text, change_text.

Cleaning: whitespace normalisation only — well-formed sentences requiring minimal preprocessing.
108 responses6 environmentsstructured open-text

Source 2 · Customers

Interviews

interview_segments.csv · 20 rows · weight 0.45

Transcribed segments from in-depth user interviews — longer, more conversational, and richest in emotional language. Hence the highest source weight. Covers first impressions, occasion mapping, and unprompted reflections on specific design elements.

Cleaning: filler word removal — you know, kind of, um, uh — to reduce noise from spoken-language transcription before emotion scoring.
20 segmentshighest weightconversational

Source 3 · Internal

Sticky Notes

sticky_notes.csv · 60 rows · weight 0.15

Physical office board feedback from internal Meta colleagues across design, engineering, and product. Very short, informal, often fragmentary — lowest signal per document but wide variety. Includes a vote_count column showing peer endorsement.

Cleaning: lowercase normalisation, abbreviation expansion (1on1 → one on one), punctuation completion for short fragments.

Research question

Which VR environments produce which emotional responses, which meeting occasions are they suited for, and which specific design attributes — materials, lighting, spatial scale — are driving those reactions?

Dual role: data scientist + UX researcher

The project sits at the intersection of NLP and UX research. Source weights reflect research judgment about data quality, not just volume. The final deliverable is a structured synthesis table for the 3D arts team — actionable, not decorative.

No labelled training data

Zero-shot NLI maps feedback to meeting occasions with no annotated examples. A self-contained NRC lexicon eliminates fragile library dependencies. The full pipeline runs on raw feedback with no manual labelling step.

The six environments

What users were reacting to

Each VR environment has a distinct material palette, lighting register, and spatial scale. The analysis asks which work — and for whom.

Executive Boardroom

Dark wood, leather, high ceilings, authoritative proportions. Scores negatively in aggregate sentiment but dominates client pitch and performance review occasions — a clear example of why raw sentiment is the wrong metric for evaluation.

trust · authority

Nature Space

Natural light, greenery, organic textures, open scale. Highest cross-source sentiment. Natural light mentions are most concentrated here — a direct signal to the 3D arts team about what to prioritise in future builds.

joy · restorative

Clean Studio

Minimal, neutral, distraction-free. Strong for deep focused solo work and all-hands presentations. The most polarising environment — users either appreciate the clarity or find it sterile.

focus · neutral

Residential Warm

Soft lighting, warm tones, domestic materials. High comfort and wellbeing scores — but consistently low focus and professionalism ratings. Right for HR and coaching; wrong for any output-driven session.

warmth · comfort

Futuristic Tech

Cool blues, geometric precision, high-tech aesthetic. Technology-sector customers respond significantly more positively than non-tech customers. This gap is invisible in aggregate — only detectable with an industry filter.

innovation · dynamic

City Loft

Industrial materials, urban scale, exposed brick and metal. Strong for brainstorming and team social occasions. A versatile mid-register space sitting between the warmth of Residential and the precision of Clean Studio.

creative · social

Methodology

How it was built

Eight NLP techniques run in sequence, each chosen to answer a specific analytical question about user emotion, occasion fit, and design attribute salience.

01

Data loading & cleaning

Source-aware cleaning — three different strategies

Each source requires different preprocessing. Surveys need only whitespace normalisation. Interviews remove spoken filler words that introduce noise into emotion scoring. Sticky notes need lowercase normalisation, abbreviation expansion, and punctuation completion for very short fragments.

✦ Keeping all three sources in a unified corpus with explicit source and weight columns means every downstream analysis can be decomposed by channel — making source disagreements visible rather than averaged away.
02

Corpus construction

Weighted triangulation across participant types

All three sources merge into a single corpus with standardised column names. Source weights reflect research quality judgment: interviews (0.45) are richest in emotional language; surveys (0.40) offer structured high-volume signal; sticky notes (0.15) are short and informal. Weighted means are used throughout for aggregate statistics.

⚠ Note: Surveys and interviews are customer voices. Sticky notes are internal colleagues. Analyses requiring only customer data — particularly the tech vs non-tech split — filter to customer sources only to avoid conflating participant types.
03

Sentiment analysis

VADER with a VR-domain lexicon extension

VADER handles informal text and short phrases well — a good fit for interview transcripts and sticky notes. Extended with 17 VR and spatial domain terms before any scoring: immersive (+2.5), grounded (+1.8), claustrophobic (−2.5), disconnected (−3.0), and more.

✦ Domain lexicon extension is the highest-leverage preprocessing step: without it, floating scores neutrally and grounding is unrecognised — both are high-frequency terms in VR user feedback.
04

Emotion scoring

Self-contained NRC lexicon — no external library

The NRC lexicon maps words to 8 emotion dimensions. Embedded as a Python dict (~160 words curated for the VR context) rather than using the fragile nrclex library. Scores are the proportion of matched emotion tokens per category, applied to the full corpus.

⚠ Note: No negation awareness — "no joy" scores positive on joy. Treat as comparative across environments. Short sticky notes scoring zero on all dimensions is expected and handled gracefully.
05

Occasion mapping

Zero-shot NLI — no labelled training data required

A pre-trained NLI model classifies each survey's occasion text into 8 meeting types (brainstorm, client pitch, deep work, team social, performance review, onboarding, all-hands, casual check-in). Multi-label with a 0.15 threshold allows a single response to match multiple occasions. A cascading fallback tries four models in order.

✦ A rule-based fallback keeps all downstream cells running if no model loads — making the notebook fully runnable without internet access or HuggingFace credentials.
06

Design attribute extraction

spaCy dependency parse — design elements → emotions

spaCy's en_core_web_sm parses each sentence. Tokens matching a curated design vocabulary (materials, colours, lighting, spatial elements) are extracted, and adjectives in the same sentence that appear in the NRC lexicon are linked as emotion words. The result is a (design element → emotion → environment) mapping.

⚠ Note: Dependency parse accuracy drops on short, informal text. Always inspect top extraction results manually — high-frequency extractions are more reliable than low-frequency ones.
07

Topic modelling

TF-IDF + NMF — what themes run through the corpus

TF-IDF (300 features, unigrams and bigrams, min_df=2) represents each document as a term-frequency vector. NMF (8 components, random_state=42) factorises into interpretable topic components, manually labelled from the top 8 keywords each.

✦ NMF over LDA here because NMF produces sparser, more interpretable topics on short-document corpora. LDA's Dirichlet prior spreads signal too evenly when documents are only 1–3 sentences long.
08

Triangulation & synthesis

Cross-source confidence scoring — separating signal from noise

Sentiment direction is computed separately per source for each environment. The agreement() function flags "full agreement" when all sources align, and "split signal" when they diverge. A composite confidence score combines cross-source variance (60%) and document count (40%), producing a per-environment reliability rating.

✦ Natural light is the only design attribute with full agreement across all three sources — making it the single most actionable finding in the entire analysis.

NLP pipeline

Eight techniques, one notebook

Each component is a deliberate choice with a specific trade-off. The full stack runs on raw user feedback with no manually labelled training data.

🎭

Primary sentiment

VADER + VR domain lexicon extension

vaderSentiment · rule-based · extended with 17 VR-specific terms before any scoring

VADER handles informal text and short phrases well — a good fit for interview transcripts and sticky notes. The base lexicon is extended with 17 domain-specific terms: immersive: +2.5, grounded: +1.8, claustrophobic: −2.5, disconnected: −3.0. Weighted means use source weights, not raw document count — so 20 interview segments contribute more signal than 60 sticky notes.

✦ The domain extension is applied before any scoring via analyser.lexicon.update(). Without it, VADER would score many high-signal VR vocabulary words as neutral — including floating, presence, and grounding.
😮

Emotion scoring

NRC Emotion Lexicon

Self-contained dict · ~160 words · curated for VR context · 8 emotion dimensions

Maps words to joy, trust, fear, surprise, sadness, disgust, anger, and anticipation. Embedded as a Python dict rather than using the nrclex library — more robust, no download required, and curated for the spatial vocabulary specific to this dataset.

⚠ No negation handling. "No joy" scores positive on joy. Use scores comparatively across environments. Short sticky notes scoring zero on all dimensions is expected behaviour, not a bug.
☁️

Vocabulary fingerprints

Word Clouds

wordcloud · per-environment · colour palettes matched to emotional register · dark background

Each environment's dominant vocabulary after stopword removal — including an extended domain-specific stopword set removing noise terms like vr, meeting, environment, session. Colour palettes match each environment's emotional register. Most useful for communicating vocabulary differences to a non-technical audience.

✦ Word size represents frequency within each environment's corpus. For discriminative vocabulary — what makes each environment linguistically distinct — the NMF topic model is more informative.
🏷

Occasion classification

Zero-Shot NLI

facebook/bart-large-mnli cascade · 8 occasion categories · multi-label · threshold 0.15

Classifies survey occasion text into 8 meeting types with no labelled examples. The framing is: "does this text entail that this space is suitable for [occasion]?" A cascading fallback tries four models in order — first that loads wins. Multi-label allows a single response to match multiple occasions.

✦ Rule-based fallback (environment → most likely occasions) keeps all downstream cells running even without internet access or HuggingFace credentials — making the notebook fully portable.
🔍

Design attribute extraction

spaCy Dependency Parse

en_core_web_sm · material / colour / lighting / spatial vocabulary · NRC adjective co-occurrence

Identifies design vocabulary tokens then looks within the same sentence for adjectives in the NRC lexicon. The (design element → emotion word → environment) triple is the core of the final synthesis deliverable for the 3D arts team.

⚠ Parse accuracy drops on short, informal text. High-frequency extractions are more reliable than low-frequency ones. Always inspect the top results before citing specific elements in recommendations.
🗂

Topic modelling

TF-IDF + NMF

TfidfVectorizer 300 features ngram 1–2 · NMF 8 components · random_state=42 · manually labelled

Discovers recurring themes across the full corpus without labelled examples. NMF produces sparser, more interpretable topics than LDA on short-document corpora. Topics are manually labelled from the top 8 keywords per component. Visualised as a heatmap (per environment) and an interactive sunburst (topic × environment).

✦ Topic assignment uses argmax on the NMF activation matrix — each document is assigned its single strongest topic. The sunburst shows how topic prevalence differs across environments.
🎯

Subgroup analysis

Tech vs Non-Tech Split

Customer corpus only · industry_class filter · Futuristic Tech gap · sentiment + emotion dimensions

Filters to customer sources only, then splits by customer_industry. The Futuristic Tech environment shows a substantial sentiment gap between technology-sector and non-tech customers — completely invisible in aggregate scores.

⚠ The tech/non-tech split is only valid for customer sources. Sticky notes (internal colleagues) are excluded from this analysis to avoid conflating two different participant populations.
🔺

Confidence & triangulation

Cross-Source Agreement Scoring

Source pivot · agreement() · confidence = 0.6 × (1/variance) + 0.4 × (doc_count / max_docs)

For each environment, sentiment direction is computed per source. The agreement() function flags "full agreement" when all three sources align, and "split signal" when they diverge. A composite confidence score combines cross-source variance (60% weight) and document count (40%). The confidence vs sentiment scatter plot shows which environments are safe to recommend versus which need more data.

✦ A split signal isn't necessarily wrong — it may reflect a genuine user-segment divide. Always check whether the split tracks a demographic variable (industry, job title, company size) before dismissing it as noise.
✦ Natural light is the only attribute with full agreement across all three sources. Every other finding has at least one source exception — making natural light the single most robust recommendation in the analysis.

Key insights & findings

What the data reveals

These are the analytical conclusions the pipeline surfaces — each one emerges from a specific notebook section and leads to a concrete recommendation for the 3D arts team.

1

Occasion fit matters more than absolute sentiment — a negative-scoring environment is not a bad environment

The Executive Boardroom scores negatively in weighted aggregate sentiment, yet it dominates the client pitch and performance review occasion categories. Users don't need to feel comfortable in the Boardroom — they need to feel the weight of the setting. This reframes how to evaluate a VR environment: the right question is not "do users like it?" but "does it match what they need to do?" → Recommendation: present environments to users with occasion context, not as stand-alone options. "This space is designed for performance reviews" changes reception entirely.

2

Natural light is the only design attribute where all three sources agree — making it the highest-leverage design lever

Surveys, interviews, and sticky notes all independently flag natural light positively, across every environment where it appears, with consistent sentiment direction. It is the only attribute with full cross-source agreement and zero split signal. Documents mentioning natural light score higher on sentiment overall and contain more presence and grounding language — suggesting light quality directly reduces VR fatigue. → Recommendation: the 3D arts team should prioritise light simulation quality above any other single design element. It is the one thing all user types agree on, across every environment.

3

The tech vs non-tech sentiment gap on Futuristic Tech is invisible in aggregate — it only appears with a segment filter

Technology-sector customers respond significantly more positively to Futuristic Tech than non-tech customers. The aggregate score sits close to neutral — masking a substantial gap between two very different user reactions. This is a direct example of why demographic segmentation is necessary even when overall scores look acceptable. → Recommendation: surface Futuristic Tech as the default environment for technology-sector accounts only. For mixed or non-tech user groups, it should be opt-in rather than default.

4

Residential Warm scores high on wellbeing but low on productivity — a systematic occasion mismatch across all sources

Residential Warm consistently produces the highest comfort and joy NRC scores. But it also consistently produces the lowest focus and professionalism ratings across surveys. Users describe it as cosy, warm, comfortable — and also as distracting, casual, not serious enough. The occasion heatmap confirms it dominates team social and onboarding categories, with near-zero score for output-driven occasions. → Recommendation: position Residential Warm explicitly as a pastoral and social environment. Any use case involving deliverables, assessments, or external stakeholders should default to a different space.

5

The confidence score is itself a finding — it tells the team which recommendations to act on now and which need more data

Not all findings in the synthesis table are equally reliable. The triangulation section assigns a confidence score per environment combining cross-source variance and document count. High confidence findings (e.g. Nature Space: positive and consistent across all sources) are direct recommendations. Split-signal findings flag segments that need deeper investigation before committing design resources. → The synthesis table is a prioritised action list, not a flat spreadsheet. Confidence scores tell the 3D arts team how much weight to give each row.

Further work

What I'd build next

Three directions that would sharpen and extend these findings — each addressing a known limitation of the current pipeline.

🤗

Replace VADER with a transformer sentiment model

VADER has no contextual understanding — "not comfortable" scores as positive on comfort. Replacing it with a lightweight transformer (e.g. distilbert-base-uncased-finetuned-sst-2) would handle negation, sarcasm, and comparative constructions correctly. The existing VR domain lexicon would become a fine-tuning dataset for domain adaptation.

📊

Merge with quantitative rating data

The synthesis table maps qualitative emotion signals to design recommendations. Merging it with the numeric survey ratings — rating_comfort, rating_focus, rating_professionalism, rating_creativity — would allow regression modelling to identify which NRC emotion dimensions predict each rating, closing the loop between NLP signal and UX outcome.

🔄

Expand to real-time in-session feedback

The current pipeline runs on post-session survey data. Integrating with in-session signals (emoji reactions, brief check-ins, physiological proxies) would enable longitudinal analysis of how emotional response shifts over a single session. The occasion mapping model could then adapt environment recommendations based on meeting duration and type in real time.

Limitations

Caveats worth knowing

🔢 Synthetic data

The three CSV files are synthetic — generated to match the structure, volume, and vocabulary distribution of real VR Workrooms feedback. Absolute scores and specific attribute frequencies should not be taken as ground truth. The value of this project is the pipeline architecture and analytical approach, both of which generalise directly to real feedback data.

📝 Short text limitations

Survey responses average 2–3 sentences. Emotion scoring on very short text is noisier than on longer prose — the NRC lexicon has limited coverage of the vocabulary that actually appears in brief feedback. Confidence scores in the synthesis table partially compensate for this, but should be read as relative rather than absolute measurements.

⚖️ Source imbalance

108 survey responses vs 20 interview segments vs 60 sticky notes is an unequal distribution. Source weights (0.40 / 0.45 / 0.15) compensate for quality differences but not for coverage gaps — if certain environments are over-represented in surveys but under-represented in interviews, the weighted aggregate still skews toward survey signal for those groups.
