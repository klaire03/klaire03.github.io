Project overview

What this project does

VR meeting spaces are not neutral containers — their materials, lighting, and scale actively shape how users feel and perform. This project asks: which environments feel right, for which meeting types, and which specific design elements are driving those emotional responses?

Research question

Which spaces produce which emotions?

Which VR environments produce which emotional responses, which meeting occasions are they suited for, and which specific design attributes — materials, lighting, spatial scale — are driving those reactions?

Dual role

Data scientist + UX researcher

The project sits at the intersection of NLP and UX research. Source weights reflect research judgment about data quality, not just volume. The final deliverable is a structured synthesis table for the 3D arts team — actionable, not decorative.

No labelled data required

Zero-shot throughout

Zero-shot NLI maps feedback to meeting occasions with no annotated examples. A self-contained NRC lexicon eliminates fragile library dependencies. The full pipeline runs on raw feedback with no manual labelling step.

The six environments

What users were reacting to

Each VR environment has a distinct material palette, lighting register, and spatial scale. The analysis asks which work — and for whom.

Executive Boardroom

trustauthority

Dark wood, leather, high ceilings, authoritative proportions. Scores negatively in aggregate sentiment but dominates client pitch and performance review occasions — a clear example of why raw sentiment is the wrong metric.

Nature Space

joyrestorative

Natural light, greenery, organic textures, open scale. Highest cross-source sentiment. Natural light mentions most concentrated here — a direct signal to the 3D arts team about what to prioritise in future builds.

Clean Studio

focusneutral

Minimal, neutral, distraction-free. Strong for deep focused solo work and all-hands presentations. The most polarising environment — users either appreciate the clarity or find it sterile.

Residential Warm

warmthcomfort

Soft lighting, warm tones, domestic materials. High comfort and wellbeing scores — but consistently low focus and professionalism ratings. Right for HR and coaching; wrong for output-driven sessions.

Futuristic Tech

innovationdynamic

Cool blues, geometric precision, high-tech aesthetic. Technology-sector customers respond significantly more positively than non-tech customers. The gap is invisible in aggregate — only detectable with an industry filter.

City Loft

creativesocial

Industrial materials, urban scale, exposed brick and metal. Strong for brainstorming and team social occasions. A versatile mid-register space sitting between the warmth of Residential and the precision of Clean Studio.

Methodology

How it was built

Eight NLP techniques run in sequence, each chosen to answer a specific analytical question about user emotion, occasion fit, and design attribute salience — all from raw feedback with no manually labelled training data.

01

Data loading & cleaning

🧹 Source-aware cleaning — three different strategies

Each source requires different preprocessing. Surveys need only whitespace normalisation. Interviews remove spoken filler words (you know, kind of, um, uh) that introduce noise into emotion scoring. Sticky notes need lowercase normalisation, abbreviation expansion (1on1 → one on one), and punctuation completion for very short fragments. All three sources merge into a single corpus with standardised column names and a participant_type flag.

✦ Keeping all three sources unified with explicit source and weight columns means every downstream analysis can be decomposed by channel — making source disagreements visible rather than averaged away.
02

Corpus construction

⚖️ Weighted triangulation across participant types

Source weights reflect research quality judgment: interviews (0.45) are richest in emotional language; surveys (0.40) offer structured high-volume signal; sticky notes (0.15) are short and informal. Weighted means are used throughout for all aggregate statistics. Analyses requiring only customer data — particularly the tech vs non-tech split — filter to customer sources only to avoid conflating participant types.

⚠ Note: Surveys and interviews are customer voices. Sticky notes are internal colleagues. The tech/non-tech split analysis excludes sticky notes entirely — mixing internal colleagues with customers would confound the segmentation.

03

Sentiment analysis

🎭 VADER + VR domain lexicon extension

VADER handles informal text and short phrases well — a good fit for interview transcripts and sticky notes. Extended with 17 VR and spatial domain terms before any scoring: immersive: +2.5, grounded: +1.8, claustrophobic: −2.5, disconnected: −3.0, and more. Applied via analyser.lexicon.update() before any scoring. Weighted means use source weights, not raw document count — so 20 interview segments contribute more signal than 60 sticky notes.

✦ The domain extension is the highest-leverage preprocessing step: without it, VADER scores floating neutrally and leaves grounding unrecognised — both high-frequency terms in VR feedback.

Fig 1 — All environments score positively, but spread is wide. Residential Warm (+0.696) leads; Clean Studio (+0.274) trails.

Fig 2 — Survey, interview, and sticky note channels compared side-by-side. Divergence flags environments where source type shapes perception.

Fig 3 — Clean Studio's violin stretches from −0.75 to +0.95 — users either value its neutrality or find it sterile.

04

Emotion scoring

😮 NRC Emotion Lexicon — self-contained, 8 dimensions

The NRC lexicon maps words to joy, trust, fear, surprise, sadness, disgust, anger, and anticipation. Embedded as a Python dict (~160 words curated for the VR context) rather than using the fragile nrclex library — more robust, no download required, and curated for the spatial vocabulary specific to this dataset. Scores are the proportion of matched emotion tokens per category.

⚠ Note: No negation awareness — "no joy" scores positive on joy. Use scores comparatively across environments. Short sticky notes scoring zero on all dimensions is expected behaviour, not a bug.

Fig 8 — Executive Boardroom peaks on trust (0.641). Futuristic Tech leads on joy (0.375). City Loft uniquely scores highest on anticipation (0.323).

Fig 9 — Overlaid radar showing each environment's 8-emotion signature. Trust-dominant Boardroom vs anticipation-dominant City Loft.

Fig 4 — Vocabulary fingerprints. Nature Space centres on natural light; Executive Boardroom on formal, client; City Loft on creative, city, view.

05

Occasion mapping

🏷 Zero-shot NLI — 8 meeting types, no labelled data

A pre-trained NLI model (facebook/bart-large-mnli cascade) classifies each survey's occasion text into 8 meeting types: brainstorm, client pitch, deep work, team social, performance review, onboarding, all-hands, casual check-in. Multi-label with a 0.15 threshold allows a single response to match multiple occasions. A cascading fallback tries four models in order — first that loads wins.

✦ A rule-based fallback (environment → most likely occasions) keeps all downstream cells running even without internet access or HuggingFace credentials — making the notebook fully portable.

Fig 5 — Executive Boardroom peaks on client_pitch (0.41) and performance_review (0.30) — explaining why its negative aggregate sentiment is a feature, not a flaw.

Fig 6 — Each bar shows which environments capture that occasion. Nature Space has the broadest spread; Residential Warm monopolises casual_checkin.

06

Design attribute extraction

🔍 spaCy dependency parse — design elements → emotions

spaCy's en_core_web_sm parses each sentence. Tokens matching a curated design vocabulary (materials, colours, lighting, spatial elements) are extracted, and adjectives in the same sentence that appear in the NRC lexicon are linked as emotion words. The result is a (design element → emotion → environment) mapping — the core of the final synthesis deliverable for the 3D arts team.

⚠ Note: Dependency parse accuracy drops on short, informal text. High-frequency extractions are more reliable than low-frequency ones. Always inspect the top results before citing specific elements in recommendations.
07

Topic modelling

🗂 TF-IDF + NMF — recurring themes across the corpus

TF-IDF (300 features, unigrams and bigrams, min_df=2) represents each document as a term-frequency vector. NMF (8 components, random_state=42) factorises into interpretable topic components, manually labelled from the top 8 keywords each. Topic assignment uses argmax on the NMF activation matrix — each document is assigned its single strongest topic. Visualised as a heatmap (per environment) and an interactive sunburst (topic × environment).

✦ NMF over LDA here because NMF produces sparser, more interpretable topics on short-document corpora. LDA's Dirichlet prior spreads signal too evenly when documents are only 1–3 sentences long.

Fig 7 — City Loft dominated by Material warmth & texture (0.81). Nature Space by Natural light & presence (0.62). Clean Studio by Spatial scale & embodiment (0.57).

08

Triangulation & synthesis

🔺 Cross-source agreement scoring — signal vs noise

Sentiment direction is computed separately per source for each environment. The agreement() function flags "full agreement" when all sources align, and "split signal" when they diverge. A composite confidence score combines cross-source variance (60%) and document count (40%), producing a per-environment reliability rating. The tech vs non-tech split filters to customer sources only, then splits by customer_industry. The Futuristic Tech environment shows a substantial sentiment gap between technology-sector and non-tech customers — invisible in aggregate scores. The synthesis table (design attribute + primary emotion + valence + top occasion + confidence) is the final deliverable for the 3D arts team.

✦ Natural light is the only design attribute with full agreement across all three sources — making it the single most actionable finding in the entire analysis. Every other finding has at least one source exception.
⚠ Note: A split signal isn't necessarily wrong — it may reflect a genuine user-segment divide. Always check whether the split tracks a demographic variable (industry, job title, company size) before dismissing it as noise.

Fig 10 — Final synthesis table: design attribute × primary emotion × valence × top occasion × confidence. Confidence scores range 0.731–0.821; rows below 0.75 warrant further data.

Key insights & findings

What the data reveals

These are the analytical conclusions the pipeline surfaces — each one emerges from a specific notebook section and leads to a concrete recommendation for the 3D arts team.

1

🎭 Occasion fit matters more than absolute sentiment — a negative-scoring environment is not a bad environment

The Executive Boardroom scores negatively in weighted aggregate sentiment, yet it dominates the client pitch and performance review occasion categories. Users don't need to feel comfortable in the Boardroom — they need to feel the weight of the setting. This reframes how to evaluate a VR environment: the right question is not "do users like it?" but "does it match what they need to do?"

→ Present environments to users with occasion context, not as stand-alone options. "This space is designed for performance reviews" changes reception entirely.

2

💡 Natural light is the only design attribute where all three sources agree — the highest-leverage design lever

Surveys, interviews, and sticky notes all independently flag natural light positively, across every environment where it appears, with consistent sentiment direction. It is the only attribute with full cross-source agreement and zero split signal. Documents mentioning natural light score higher on sentiment overall and contain more presence and grounding language — suggesting light quality directly reduces VR fatigue.

→ The 3D arts team should prioritise light simulation quality above any other single design element. It is the one thing all user types agree on, across every environment.

3

🔬 The tech vs non-tech sentiment gap on Futuristic Tech is invisible in aggregate — it only appears with a segment filter

Technology-sector customers respond significantly more positively to Futuristic Tech than non-tech customers. The aggregate score sits close to neutral — masking a substantial gap between two very different user reactions. This is a direct example of why demographic segmentation is necessary even when overall scores look acceptable.

→ Surface Futuristic Tech as the default environment for technology-sector accounts only. For mixed or non-tech user groups, it should be opt-in rather than default.

4

🏠 Residential Warm scores high on wellbeing but low on productivity — a systematic occasion mismatch

Residential Warm consistently produces the highest comfort and joy NRC scores. But it also consistently produces the lowest focus and professionalism ratings across surveys. Users describe it as cosy and warm — and also as distracting, casual, and not serious enough. The occasion heatmap confirms it dominates team social and onboarding categories, with near-zero score for output-driven occasions.

→ Position Residential Warm explicitly as a pastoral and social environment. Any use case involving deliverables, assessments, or external stakeholders should default to a different space.

5

📊 The confidence score is itself a finding — it tells the team which recommendations to act on now

Not all findings in the synthesis table are equally reliable. The triangulation section assigns a confidence score per environment combining cross-source variance and document count. High confidence findings (Nature Space: positive and consistent across all sources) are direct recommendations. Split-signal findings flag segments that need deeper investigation before committing design resources.

→ The synthesis table is a prioritised action list, not a flat spreadsheet. Confidence scores tell the 3D arts team how much weight to give each row. Rows below 0.75 warrant further data before acting.

Further work

What I'd build next

Three directions that would sharpen and extend these findings — each addressing a known limitation of the current pipeline.

🤗

Replace VADER with a transformer sentiment model

VADER has no contextual understanding — "not comfortable" scores as positive on comfort. Replacing it with a lightweight transformer (e.g. distilbert-base-uncased-finetuned-sst-2) would handle negation, sarcasm, and comparative constructions correctly. The existing VR domain lexicon would become a fine-tuning dataset.

📊

Merge with quantitative rating data

The synthesis table maps qualitative emotion signals to design recommendations. Merging with numeric survey ratings — rating_comfort, rating_focus, rating_professionalism — would allow regression modelling to identify which NRC emotion dimensions predict each rating, closing the loop between NLP signal and UX outcome.

🔄

Expand to real-time in-session feedback

The current pipeline runs on post-session survey data. Integrating with in-session signals (emoji reactions, brief check-ins, physiological proxies) would enable longitudinal analysis of how emotional response shifts over a single session. The occasion mapping model could then adapt environment recommendations based on meeting duration and type in real time.

Limitations

Caveats worth knowing

🔢 Synthetic data

The three CSV files are synthetic — generated to match the structure, volume, and vocabulary distribution of real VR Workrooms feedback. Absolute scores and specific attribute frequencies should not be taken as ground truth. The value is the pipeline architecture and analytical approach, both of which generalise directly to real feedback data.

📝 Short text limitations

Survey responses average 2–3 sentences. Emotion scoring on very short text is noisier than on longer prose — the NRC lexicon has limited coverage of the vocabulary that actually appears in brief feedback. Confidence scores in the synthesis table partially compensate, but should be read as relative rather than absolute measurements.

⚖️ Source imbalance

108 survey responses vs 20 interview segments vs 60 sticky notes is an unequal distribution. Source weights compensate for quality differences but not for coverage gaps — if certain environments are over-represented in surveys but under-represented in interviews, the weighted aggregate still skews toward survey signal for those groups.
