Project overview

What this project does

Smartwatch customers are not a single homogeneous group. Fitness enthusiasts, connectivity-focused professionals, and casual users have entirely different motivations, expectations, and price sensitivities. Treating them as one audience leads to ineffective marketing and product positioning.

Owners · n=769

Identify distinct user types and the behavioural and attitudinal signals that drive satisfaction or predict churn. Each persona was profiled using lift analysis — how it deviates from the population average across all 51 features.

Non-owners · n=227

Identify prospective buyer segments and the specific motivations, lifestyle signals, and brand interests that distinguish each group — and what messaging would most likely convert them.

Methodology

How it was built

K-Means is unsupervised — it finds natural groupings without pre-labelled training data, making it well-suited for exploratory segmentation. Several deliberate decisions were made to get the best possible clusters from survey data.

01

Setup

Split owners and non-owners

Non-owners had no data at all for watch satisfaction, spend, wear frequency, or churn — those questions simply weren't asked of them. Clustering both groups together would mean inventing values for 23% of the sample. The two groups also answer different analytical questions: owners → loyalty and churn risk; non-owners → barriers to first purchase.

⚠ Note: Splitting leaves only 227 non-owners. Three of the four non-owner clusters have ~28 people each — findings are directional, not definitive.
02

Encoding

Ordinal encoding over one-hot

35+ survey columns use Likert scales with a real order — Not at all important through to Extremely important. One-hot encoding destroys that structure: the model would treat each response as an unrelated category with no sense of proximity. Ordinal encoding (0–4) preserves rank, giving K-Means something meaningful to measure distance against.

✦ This single change raised the silhouette score from 0.06 → 0.22, a 3.5× improvement in cluster quality.
03

Encoding

Ordinal vs one-hot — knowing which to use

Not all categorical columns deserve the same treatment. Brand names (phone brand, watch brand, OS, churn reason, living situation) have no natural order — they're just different labels. These were one-hot encoded. The key decision is knowing which method applies to which column, not applying the same approach to everything.

⚠ Note: drop_first=False is intentional. Dropping a dummy column prevents multicollinearity in regression — irrelevant for clustering. Keeping all categories gives the model more complete information.
04

Imputation & Scaling

Median imputation, not zero

K-Means cannot handle NaN values. Zero means lowest possible answer on an ordinal scale — filling missing values with zero would falsely imply every gap was a worst-case response. The column median is neutral and doesn't push clusters toward extremes. StandardScaler was then applied: without it, a column ranging 2017–2021 (purchase year) would dominate one ranging 0–4 (importance rating) purely due to numeric scale.

⚠ Note: The scaler was fit separately on owners and non-owners. Fitting on the combined dataset before splitting would be data leakage.
05

Clustering

Choosing K — interpretability over metric peak

Both the elbow method (inertia) and silhouette score were tested across K=2–8 for owners and K=2–6 for non-owners. K=5 (owners) and K=4 (non-owners) were chosen — not because they had the best scores. K=8 scored 0.290 vs K=5's 0.217 for owners, but produced clusters as small as 15 people. For non-owners, K=5 scored 0.620 vs K=4's 0.514, but created one group of 114 surrounded by four groups of 28 — outlier artefacts, not real segments.

⚠ Note: Silhouette score alone is misleading. Always check cluster sizes alongside the metric before committing to K.
06

Profiling & Naming

Lift analysis + manual naming

Each cluster was profiled by subtracting the overall population mean from the cluster mean on every feature. A lift of +1.89 on spent_watch means that cluster spends nearly 2 standard deviations above average — instantly interpretable. Features were ranked by mean absolute lift across all clusters to identify the main drivers. Persona names were then assigned manually based on the full combination of positive and negative signals, not just the single highest-lift feature.

07

Visualisation

Four complementary charts — PCA + UMAP, heatmap, bar chart, radar

With 80 features for owners and 47 for non-owners, clusters can't be plotted directly. PCA reduced dimensionality to 30 components, then UMAP projected to 2D for scatter plotting — each dot one respondent, coloured by cluster. A lift heatmap (15 features × 5 clusters, red/blue centred at 0) shows all personas simultaneously. A horizontal bar chart ranks the top 10 features by mean absolute lift. A normalised radar chart (MinMaxScaler to 0–1) shows the overall shape of each persona across the 6 most differentiating features.

⚠ Note: UMAP axes have no interpretable meaning. It's a visual sanity check — do the clusters look separated or do they blur together? Different random seeds produce different-looking layouts from identical clusters.

Full pipeline

# Pipeline — owners and non-owners run separately through identical steps
1. Load & clean — convert string 'NA' to nulls; fix UTF-8 encoding bug in frequency_wear
2. Split groups — owners (n=769) and non-owners (n=227) analysed independently
3. Ordinal encoding — 13 custom scale maps preserving rank order across 35+ columns
4. One-hot encoding — nominal columns only: brand, OS, churn reason, living situation
5. Median imputation — neutral fill; avoids implying worst-case responses for missing values
6. StandardScaler — fit separately per group to avoid data leakage
7. K selection — elbow + silhouette tested; final K chosen on interpretability, not metric peak
8. KMeans fit — random_state=42, n_init='auto'
9. Lift analysis — cluster mean − population mean; ranked by mean absolute lift
10. Manual naming — based on top positive + negative lift signals per cluster
11. Visualisation — PCA→UMAP scatter, lift heatmap, bar chart, normalised radar
12. Export — owners_segmented.csv · nonowners_segmented.csv

Key insights & recommendations

What the data actually says

Insights and marketing recommendations are combined here — each finding leads directly to what should be done about it.

1

Most owners are premium-oriented, not value-driven

40% of owners are Premium & Connected Buyers — the highest-spending, highest-intent segment — and another 22% are Active Brand Explorers actively looking to upgrade from a budget device. Over 60% of owners have genuine upgrade potential. The narrative that the smartwatch market is dominated by price-sensitive buyers doesn't hold up in this data. → Lead with ecosystem and safety (emergency SOS, smart home, Apple/Android sync) for the premium segment. Run trade-in campaigns comparing budget devices to mid-range alternatives for upgraders eyeing Fossil and Samsung.

2

Fitness is not a universal motivation — not even close

Three of five owner clusters and two of four non-owner clusters show weak or negative fitness signals. Passive & Disengaged owners are satisfied without any health engagement; Productivity-Driven Professionals actively reject the fitness angle. A fitness-first marketing strategy reaches maybe 30–40% of this market. → Passive & Disengaged respond only to tap-to-pay and commuter messaging. Productivity-Driven Professionals convert on calendar alerts, Siri, and hands-free calls — not step counts.

3

The biggest non-owner group has no single clear barrier

Health-Motivated Actives (63% of non-owners) are already doing the activities a smartwatch is designed to support. Their modest lift values — nothing above +1.17 — suggest no dominant pain point. The issue is general: awareness, price uncertainty, and unclear product fit. → Broad benefit-led campaigns across brands and price points. Simple value messaging: 'here's what tracking actually does for your health.' Free trials or money-back guarantees reduce commitment friction.

4

Two non-owner groups are ready to convert right now

Productivity-Driven Professionals already want Apple and already value the features Apple Watch delivers best. Mindful & Mobile have a clear wellness use case that maps directly onto Fitbit Sense or Garmin wellness features. Together these two groups are 25% of non-owners — and they don't need convincing on the category, just the right product message. → Professionals: Apple Watch SE, positioned as a professional tool, via LinkedIn. Mindful & Mobile: wellness positioning (sleep, stress, breathing, smart home) via Garmin/Fitbit channels. No performance messaging.

5

Android Everyday Users are a retention problem, not an upsell one

The second-largest owner segment (26%) has the lowest spend, the lowest target upgrade price, and the lowest engagement with premium features of any group. Upsell campaigns on this segment will cost more than they return. → Practical, ecosystem-focused retention messaging (Pixel compatibility, navigation, Google Maps integration) with a loyalty offer at renewal. Don't invest in premium campaign spend on this group.

Further work

What I'd build next

Three directions that would sharpen and extend these findings.

🎯

Cluster membership classifier

Train a logistic regression or Random Forest on the cluster labels to identify the features most predictive of segment membership. The output would be a lightweight scoring model that could classify new survey respondents into a persona without running the full clustering pipeline — useful for real-time segmentation at scale.

📉

Churn prediction model

Use frequency_wear and watch_satisfaction as target variables for a churn model within the owner group. Which individual features best predict a customer becoming Passive & Disengaged? This would turn the segmentation from descriptive to predictive.

🧪

A/B test the conversion hypotheses

The Productivity-Driven Professional and Mindful & Mobile conversion hypotheses are directional — they're based on stated preference data, not behavioural outcomes. A/B testing persona-targeted messaging (professional tool vs. wellness device framing) against the Health-Motivated Actives control would validate whether the segmentation translates to real conversion lift.

Limitations

Caveats worth knowing

📊 Silhouette scores are moderate

Owner clusters score 0.217 at K=5 — not high, but typical for real-world survey data where people naturally sit somewhere between archetypes. Treat personas as dominant tendencies, not rigid boxes. Real people overlap at the boundaries.

🔬 Non-owner sample is small

227 non-owners split into four groups leaves three clusters with only ~28 people each. The directional findings are valid, but decisions involving significant budget should wait for a larger sample. A follow-up survey targeted at non-owners would strengthen confidence significantly.

💬 Stated preference data

People overstate feature interest and understate price sensitivity in surveys. Behavioural data — actual purchases, return rates, app usage, wear frequency from device logs — would sharpen and validate these findings. This analysis is a strong starting point, not a finished picture.
