Moody's Analytics · Reporting

Reporting Platform
Redesign

Reframing a reporting tool as a service — and shifting the org from validating ideas to asking better questions.

ContextRisk & compliance platform · B2B SaaS

Background

The product is a reporting and analytics feature inside a risk & compliance platform, used by two groups:

  • Analysts use it to track the status and outcomes of client screening cases
  • Operations leaders use it to monitor team performance and spot where bottlenecks form.

Challenge

The reporting feature leaned heavily on data engineering — every bespoke dashboard had to be custom-built by hand. That meant slow delivery, high cost and stubbornly low adoption. And yet the team's instinct was that the answer was simply more dashboards.

Research questions

  • What shapes how much, and how, people rely on the reporting feature day to day?
  • Where in the end-to-end service does the workflow actually break down?
  • When the reporting data doesn't meet compliance users' needs, how do they get the job done instead?

How I worked

01

Understand the problem space

Map real usage before assuming a solution.

Approach

  • Reviewed usage patterns and dashboard interaction data
  • Analysed customer feedback and support queries

What it showed

  • The starting assumption — that users wanted more dashboards — didn't hold. They were avoiding the ones they already had.
02

Deep-dive into user needs

Find where trust and workflow actually break.

Approach

  • Customer interviews on the real workflow around dashboards
  • Probed where trust in data breaks down and what workarounds reveal
  • Contextual walkthroughs to locate friction and errors

What it showed

  • Graphs lacked interactivity — no percentages, labels, values or breakdowns
  • Data felt static — no trend lines or contextual comparisons
  • No audit trail — compliance users couldn't see when data was refreshed, or by whom
03

Service blueprint

See the end-to-end service, not just the screen.

Approach

  • Workshop with Account Managers, IPLs, Engineers, Compliance SMEs and Analysts (6–8)
  • Pre-read circulated; Miro board structured by service stage
  • Mapped frontstage actions, backstage systems, pain points and metrics across seven stages

How it helped

  • Gave PMs, engineers, compliance SMEs and analysts one shared view of the end-to-end service — so debates over whose problem it was turned into agreement on where the friction actually sat
  • Shifted the conversation from individual screens to the service as a whole, aligning the team on what to tackle first
04

Prioritisation

Get the right work onto the roadmap.

Approach

  • Readout structured as Issue → Impact → Recommendation → Feasibility → Business objective
  • Scored each pain-point cluster on impact and feasibility
  • Framed findings as activation, retention and engagement risks — not UX problems

How it helped

  • Gave stakeholders a shared, objective basis to deprioritise dashboard expansion in favour of transparency features
  • Turned a long wishlist into a sequenced, defensible roadmap — each call backed by a business rationale leadership could stand behind

Insights & recommendations

Users didn't want more dashboards — they avoided the ones they had.

Recommendation

Stop adding dashboards; make the existing ones trustworthy and worth opening.

The real gaps were interactivity, data context and an audit trail — trust, not volume.

Recommendation

Add interactivity, contextual comparisons and a visible data-refresh audit trail before building anything new.

Named as compliance and adoption risk, the findings moved onto the roadmap.

Recommendation

Frame every finding by the risk it carries — compliance, adoption, retention — so it competes for roadmap space.

Stakeholder requests shifted from “validate this” to “what should we be asking?”

Recommendation

Keep research upstream of decisions: shape the questions, don't just validate built work.

Lessons learnt

  • Measure before you research. Instrument the metrics before fieldwork starts, not after.
  • Name the stake, not the symptom. “No audit trail” is a symptom; “compliance risk” is the stake. Framing findings by what they threaten is what gets research onto the roadmap.
