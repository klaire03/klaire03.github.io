Business goal

Why this project exists

The goal was to simulate a production-grade churn analytics framework for a SaaS cybersecurity product — moving from raw operational data through descriptive reporting to a predictive system that quantifies revenue at risk. Three questions framed every decision: what behaviour predicts churn, who is at risk today, and how much revenue is exposed?

The data covered account profiles, subscription history, feature usage, support interactions, and churn events. Getting useful answers required building the full stack — cleaning and modelling the data in SQL, surfacing it in Tableau dashboards, and finally feeding it into a machine learning pipeline.

Phase 1 — Data Engineering

Building the SQL foundation

Before any analysis, the raw relational schema needed transforming into something analytical tools could trust. The source data lived across five tables — accounts, subscriptions, feature_usage, churn_events, and support_tickets — connected through foreign keys but full of inconsistent date formats, text-encoded booleans, and unnormalised flags. The work happened in three layers: clean first, aggregate second, then produce a single master mart row per account.

Before reaching for machine learning, a rule-based SQL risk model was built to test whether simple weighted signals could separate churners from non-churners. The mart_account_risk view assigned point scores to behavioural flags — low usage, high errors, support escalations, low CSAT, recent downgrades, disabled auto-renew — and segmented accounts into three tiers.

HIGH RISK

churn_risk_score ≥ 8

MEDIUM RISK

Validating the tiers against actual churn rates revealed limited separation between segments — a useful negative result. Rather than manually recalibrating the weights, this finding became the data-driven case for progressing to machine learning.

View code — mart_account_risk scoring model SQL
CREATE OR REPLACE VIEW mart_account_risk AS SELECT m.*, -- Weighted behavioural + contract signals CASE WHEN m.total_usage < 50 THEN 2 ELSE 0 END + CASE WHEN m.total_errors > 20 THEN 2 ELSE 0 END + CASE WHEN m.escalations >= 1 THEN 2 ELSE 0 END + CASE WHEN m.avg_csat <= 2 THEN 2 ELSE 0 END + CASE WHEN s.downgrade_flag = 'true' THEN 3 ELSE 0 END + CASE WHEN s.auto_renew_flag = 'false'THEN 3 ELSE 0 END AS churn_risk_score FROM mart_account_health m LEFT JOIN subscriptions s ON m.account_id = s.account_id;

Predictive accuracy isn't enough on its own. For a model to change real behaviour, stakeholders need to understand why it flagged a particular account. Feature importance revealed the population-level drivers — auto-renew status, downgrade history, error frequency, support escalations. SHAP values went further, quantifying each feature's contribution to each individual prediction.

"This account's 72% churn risk is primarily driven by downgrade history and disabled auto-renew."

SHAP transforms a black-box number into an actionable narrative. A customer success team can respond to a reason — not just a score.

The final step moved beyond binary churn predictions. Each active account received a churn probability, which was multiplied by its MRR to produce a revenue-at-risk figure. This changes the intervention queue entirely: a medium-probability account at £140k MRR represents more exposure than a high-probability account at £8k.

Revenue at Risk = P(Churn) × MRR

Converts model output into financial exposure. Enables revenue-weighted retention prioritisation and ROI-driven intervention planning — rather than chasing the highest probability scores.

Key findings & recommendations

What the data says

Two themes emerged: the operational and behavioural drivers of churn, and the commercial picture that determines where retention effort creates the most revenue value.

1

22% churn rate — retention is the primary growth lever

With acquisition steady at 15–30 signups per period and not accelerating, the compounding effect of reducing churn outweighs new logo acquisition at current growth rates. Every retained account is revenue that doesn't need replacing.

Model the revenue impact of a 2–3 pp churn reduction before committing further acquisition budget.

2

Churn rises with tenure: 18% → 27%

Longer-tenured accounts churn at higher rates — a signal of weak long-term value expansion. The product delivers initial value but appears to struggle growing with customer needs in the 24–50 month window.

Introduce mid-lifecycle health checks and expansion playbooks for accounts approaching the 24-month mark.

3

Pro downgrades (36%) signal contraction before churn

Downgrades are a leading indicator. The 36% downgrade rate among Pro accounts points to value perception issues — customers are contracting before they leave entirely, giving a clear intervention window.

Trigger proactive outreach and a value review conversation immediately after any downgrade event, not at renewal.

4

Churn is partly economic — pricing is a top driver

Alongside support friction and product gaps, pricing and budget feature prominently among churn reasons. This means retention strategy needs commercial flexibility, not just product or service improvements.

Test flexible pricing, annual commitment incentives, or tailored packaging for price-sensitive at-risk accounts.

5

87-hour first response and 19% escalations are churn signals

Support failure isn't just a satisfaction issue — both metrics ranked as significant churn drivers in the XGBoost feature importance analysis. Operational friction translates directly to customer exits.

Set tighter SLA targets for high-MRR accounts and implement escalation monitoring with automated senior routing.

6

Revenue-weighted prioritisation is non-negotiable

High-MRR accounts create disproportionate exposure even at moderate churn probability. The scoring table shows a "Low" risk account with £138k MRR ranked 4th by actual revenue exposure — above several "High" risk accounts.

Sort the intervention queue by P(Churn) × MRR, not by churn probability alone.

1

Basic tier drives the largest total ARR

Despite lower per-account value, Basic tier volume makes it the dominant ARR contributor. Even small improvements in Basic retention compound into material revenue impact at scale — more so than optimising Enterprise accounts alone.

Invest in scalable, low-touch retention programmes at the Basic tier rather than high-touch enterprise-style interventions.

2

FinTech is the highest-revenue vertical

FinTech accounts generate the most ARR, meaning churn in this segment carries outsized financial impact. A cluster of FinTech churns in a single quarter would significantly affect the revenue base.

Assign dedicated retention monitoring to high-value industries — FinTech, DevTools, and Cybersecurity.

3

France is the strongest geographic churn predictor

Country emerged as a significant feature in the XGBoost model, with France ranking as the top geographic churn predictor. This suggests localised pricing, competitive, or localisation factors beyond what global product and support improvements would address.

Conduct a France regional deep-dive: competitive benchmarking, pricing analysis, and localisation gaps.

Further work

What I'd build next

Three directions that would sharpen and extend these findings.

🔁

Churn prediction at the event level

The current model operates on lifetime account aggregates. Rebuilding it on time-windowed features — rolling 90-day usage, recent ticket velocity — would detect deterioration as it happens rather than retrospectively.

💰

Revenue simulation model

Model the MRR impact of reducing churn by 1, 2, and 5 percentage points under different acquisition scenarios. This turns the analysis from descriptive to financial planning — and makes the business case for retention spend concrete.

🧪

A/B test the intervention hypotheses

The recommendation to trigger outreach after downgrades and improve SLA response for high-MRR accounts is directional. Running controlled tests on these interventions would validate whether the model's feature signals translate to actual retention lift.

Limitations

Caveats worth knowing

📊 Simulated dataset

The underlying data is synthetic — designed to reflect realistic SaaS patterns but not derived from a live product. The modelling approach and analytical decisions are production-grade; the specific numbers are illustrative.

⚖️ Class imbalance handled, not eliminated

At 22% churn, the dataset is moderately imbalanced. scale_pos_weight and PR-AUC prioritisation address this, but real-world deployment would require threshold calibration against actual cost-of-misclassification data.

💬 Stated reasons vs. behavioural signals

Churn reason codes are self-reported and often imprecise. Behavioural data — actual usage patterns, login cadence, feature adoption — would sharpen and validate these findings. This analysis is a strong starting point, not a finished picture.
