Upsell Journey
Optimisation

Optimising the offer that moves CCleaner’s free users up to Pro — a channel worth around £950K ($1.2M) a month.

Background

CCleaner is a free PC clean-up tool with a very large user base. During its update flow, free users were shown an offer to upgrade to CCleaner Pro — automatic cleaning, real-time monitoring and premium support. The project optimised those upgrade screens to lift conversion, upgrade volume and revenue.

Challenge

This was an upsell within one product — moving free users up to the paid tier:

  • CCleaner Free — the manual, on-demand clean-up people already use and trust.
  • CCleaner Pro — the paid upgrade: automatic cleaning, real-time monitoring and premium support.

Telemetry showed weak conversion to Pro and low follow-through to payment. The question was what would move satisfied free users to pay for automation they hadn’t felt they needed.

Research questions

  • What attitudes, habits and trust shape how CCleaner Free users respond to the Pro upgrade offer?
  • Which segments are most likely to ignore or skip the offer, and why?
  • Is weak conversion a desire problem, or a UI, clarity and pricing problem?
  • Which variants of the offer flow drive the strongest acceptance and upgrade volume?

How I worked

01

Hypothesis framework

Make every test a falsifiable bet.

Approach

  • Pooled questions into a shared sheet from two angles — behavioural analytics (how people moved through the offer: clicks, dwell, where they dropped off) and transactional data (accept rates, conversions to paid, payment drop-off)
  • Labelled each by urgency (high / medium / low) and mapped it to the screen it affected
  • Rewrote each as a clear, testable hypothesis

How it helped

  • Gave the team a shared, prioritised backlog of testable hypotheses — so the A/B programme tackled the highest-urgency questions first rather than whatever was easiest to build
  • Made results decisive: because each hypothesis was falsifiable, every test returned a clear yes or no instead of an ambiguous read
02

Behavioural segmentation

Find who skips the offer — and who’s receptive.

Approach

  • Clustered users on in-product behaviour — offer-screen engagement, dwell, drop-off and prior CCleaner usage — to surface natural segments
  • Profiled each segment by how it responded to the Pro upgrade offer

What it showed

  • A few segments drove most of the skips, so optimisation could target them instead of treating every user the same
03

Survey: attitudes & trust

Understand the why behind the behaviour.

Approach

  • Surveyed a sample representative of the CCleaner Free base on attitudes to paying for software, trust in the offer and buying habits
  • Compared responses across the behavioural segments (correlation tests) to tie attitude to behaviour

What it showed

  • Resistance was mostly about value and clarity, not lack of trust — people didn’t see what Pro added or why it was worth paying for, rather than distrusting it
04

A/B & usability testing

Test the hypotheses and find the strongest offer flow.

Approach

  • Asynchronous usability testing via UsabilityHub — 50 participants (UK & US), ages 20–75
  • A/B tests in Optimizely measuring click-through, dwell, acceptance and upgrade volume
  • Each test mapped back to a prioritised hypothesis from the framework

What it showed

  • UI prominence — the accept action wasn’t visible enough, causing hesitation
  • Value clarity — users couldn’t see what they’d get, which bred mistrust
  • Pricing communication — unclear pricing quietly blocked conversion
05

Readout & delivery

Make the findings land with every audience.

Approach

  • Tailored the readout three ways — a one-pager for engineering, a deck for leadership, a full report for depth
  • So each audience got the findings in the format they’d actually act on

Insights & recommendations

The action to take the offer wasn’t prominent enough — a UI problem, not a desire problem.

Recommendation

Make the accept action unmistakable — prominence and hierarchy, not a louder pitch.

Users couldn’t see what Pro added over Free, so they had no reason to act.

Recommendation

Show what Pro adds over Free, plainly — clear, concrete value is what converts.

Unclear pricing was quietly blocking monetisation.

Recommendation

Show pricing clearly and early — ambiguity costs conversions silently.

Lessons learnt

  • An experiment tells you which, not why. The A/B programme found the winning offer flow, but the survey and usability work explained why satisfied free users hesitated to upgrade — pairing the two is what made the wins repeatable, not lucky.
  • You’re competing with “good enough”. Free users weren’t dissatisfied — they just didn’t see why to pay. The gains came from making Pro’s added value and pricing obvious, not from pushing harder.
