Google · Pixel Watch

Pixel Watch
& Wear OS

Pre-launch research for the first Pixel Watch — bringing Fitbit into Wear OS without losing Fitbit's users. The differentiator wasn't features; it was aligning mental models.

Background

The first Pixel Watch — pre-launch and unannounced, hardware under NDA. The brief was to bring Fitbit into Wear OS 3 (co-built with Samsung and Fitbit) and ship Google's first watch built entirely in-house — the smartwatch equivalent of a Pixel phone.

Challenge

Two jobs at once: carry existing Fitbit users into the new ecosystem without losing them, and define a "pure Google" watch that could compete with Apple and Samsung. Where could a Google watch win? Deep Fitbit integration — health and fitness native to Wear OS for the first time — became the wedge.

Research questions

  • Who are we building for, and what do they want from a smartwatch — how should it relate to their phone and health data?
  • Where can a Google watch win against competitors, and how seamlessly does Fitbit integration fit into the Wear OS experience?
  • Can users get through the make-or-break journeys — pairing, starting a workout, handling a notification, customising faces and tiles?

How I worked

01

Competitor benchmark

Map the field and find where a Google watch could win.

Approach

  • Heuristic walkthrough of Apple Watch, Galaxy Watch 4 and Fitbit, scoring each on the core journeys
  • Built a feature-and-experience matrix to pinpoint the gap Google could own

What it showed

  • Android users felt underserved vs Apple’s tight hardware–software–health integration
  • Pointed to depth of health tracking and seamless Fitbit integration as the differentiator — not watch faces, which set the focus for the survey
02

Attitudes & segmentation survey

Map who we’re building for, by motivation and attitude.

Approach

  • Designed off the benchmark and existing user understanding — measuring health motivation, attitudes and watch habits with validated rating batteries
  • Sampled across internal dogfooders, current smartwatch owners and non-owners (n ≈ 300–800), screened by ownership so habit could be separated from preference
  • Analysis: k-means clustering in Python to find the natural groups (chosen for interpretable, distance-based segments on the attitudinal items)
  • Mapped clusters onto existing personas (Excel / VLOOKUP) to make them actionable for the team

What it showed

  • Four motivation-based segments emerged: Serious-fitness, Health-curious casual, Glance-and-notifications-first, and Quantified-self
  • Gave the team a sized, shared picture of the user base — and the recruitment frame for the sessions that followed
03

Segment interviews + usability

Go deep with each segment and test the make-or-break journeys.

Approach

  • Recruited from the survey segments, so every session represented a real motivation group
  • Generative interview first, then task-based usability on prototype Wear OS — interview before the prototype so it didn’t prime their answers
  • Make-or-break journeys under test: pairing, starting a workout, notifications, faces and tiles
  • Iterative rounds (5–8 per round — internal dogfooders plus a small NDA’d panel); think-aloud, task-success and ease ratings, severity-rated issues; Fitbit tested in real conditions (treadmill, multitasking)

What it showed

  • The gap was mental models, not features — Fitbit-habit users and Wear OS power users wanted different things from the same watch
  • Fixes re-tested the following round to confirm they held

Insights & recommendations

The problem wasn't missing features — it was misaligned mental models.

Recommendation

Reframe the brief from feature integration to experience adaptation.

Reduce onboarding overload so first-run doesn't overwhelm.

Fitbit users want passive tracking, simplicity and habits; Wear OS users want a feature-rich, interactive, ecosystem-driven device.

Recommendation

Preserve familiar Fitbit behaviours rather than overwrite them.

Android users felt underserved versus Apple's tight hardware–software–health integration.

Recommendation

Design clearer multi-app ecosystem navigation.

The differentiator was depth of health tracking and seamless Fitbit integration — not watch faces.

Recommendation

Simplify the key journeys — Maps, Fitbit, notifications and music.

Impact

4 personas, one language
Adopted across the team

The four health-motivation personas became the shared reference across PM, design and engineering — and the targeting frame for recruiting every study that followed.

Pre-launch fixes
Make-or-break journeys

First-run pairing, starting a workout and notification handling were redesigned off the usability findings before the watch shipped.

Reframed the brief
Problem redefined

Research moved the team from ‘which features to integrate’ to ‘how to match existing mental models’ — changing what got built, not just how it looked.

Lessons learnt

  • Design personas to be maintained, not just delivered. They got adopted across PM, design and engineering, but I left no owner or refresh cadence — so they risked going stale as the product evolved. I’d build that in from day one.
  • Share as you go, not all at once. Regular debriefs and live observation throughout the study kept stakeholders bought in and let fixes start mid-research — rather than landing everything in one final readout they could nod at and park.
