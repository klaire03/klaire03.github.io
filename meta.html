VR Workrooms
Spatial Research

Researching a product category with no precedent — where the question wasn't "does this work" but "what does working even mean here?"

Background

Meta's Workrooms is a VR productivity app — customisable personal offices and avatar-based meeting spaces. A genuinely new product category with no established norms or benchmarks.

Challenge

Analytics showed low engagement with the app's tools and high drop-off during onboarding.

Research questions

  • In a category with no precedent, what does “working” actually mean to people in VR?
  • Why do users drop off during onboarding, and where does the core value fail to land?
  • Which tools and interactions are users avoiding — is that discoverability or difficulty?
  • What presence, customisation and accessibility factors decide whether VR feels usable for real work?

How I worked

01

Forming hypotheses

Let the data point before talking to users.

Approach

  • Analysed VR navigation, engagement (repeat usage, active users, session duration, drop-off), onboarding funnel, latency and crashes
  • Cross-referenced with the Workrooms Facebook community (social listening)

What it showed

  • Core value isn't communicated early or clearly enough
  • Interaction with tools is unclear or complex
  • Limited customisation and control over the workspace
02

Interviews & usability

Understand the VR workspace experience and onboarding.

Approach

  • Hybrid sessions: exploratory interviews + task-based usability
  • 8 participants (4 existing users, 4 non-users)
  • Accessibility testing with visually impaired users

What it showed

  • Initial friction navigating and interacting in VR
  • Low feature discoverability
  • Limited customisation reduced perceived value
  • Accessibility gaps in contrast and visual clarity
03

Focus groups

Explore collaboration behaviour and perceived value vs existing tools.

Approach

  • 3 focus groups, mixed professional backgrounds (dogfooding)
  • Observed group collaboration, communication patterns and tool usage
  • Facilitated discussion on experiences and expectations

What it showed

  • Strong need for presence and avatar realism
  • Unclear fit within existing workflows
  • Opportunity to communicate value and simplify onboarding
04

Readout & co-design

Turn findings into prioritised, buildable change.

Approach

  • Readout as Issue → Impact → Recommendation → Feasibility → Business objective
  • Affinity mapping, card sorting for IA and wireframing with the team
  • Co-designed screen manipulation, expanded VR environments and a redesigned Personal Office

Insights & recommendations

Low onboarding completion drove high drop-off and a weak first impression.

Recommendation

Rebuild onboarding to reach first value fast and cut the first-run drop-off.

Low feature discoverability left key tools underused.

Recommendation

Surface core tools in context so people find them without hunting.

Limited customisation reduced perceived usefulness.

Recommendation

Let people shape their space — customisation is what makes the workspace feel useful.

Accessibility gaps were excluding users from the start.

Recommendation

Design for accessibility from the first build — contrast, captions and comfort — not as a retrofit.

Impact

Repeat usage
Early engagement

Strong engagement with VR meetings and tools, with early repeat usage in beta-cohort analytics.

Presence
Spatial setup

Positive feedback on the spatial setup and sense of presence in VR.

Rolling research
Embedded practice

Cross-functional demand grew — teams began commissioning their own rolling research.

Lessons learnt

  • No benchmark? Build the frame. In a new category, research has to define what “working” means before it can measure it.
  • Research as infrastructure. The clearest success signal was teams commissioning their own research — the practice had embedded itself, not just one researcher.
